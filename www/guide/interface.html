The interface

A tour of the screen: three zones, and what each part does. Once you know the zones, everything in Daimond has a place.

The rail — left

The rail is split by a divider you can drag but not remove. Above the divider is what you are working on; below it is the admin panel.

Facets

The pursuits you keep. A Facet holds what a chat taught you about a piece of work, so the next stretch of it starts from what you know. Search and tag them as they accumulate. Add one with the ＋ beside Facets.

Chats

Your conversations. Start one with the ＋ beside Chats; click any chat to bring it back to the centre.

Facets and chats are covered in full on Chats & Facets.

The admin panel

Below the divider, the admin panel is the state of the machine at a glance. Settings live here rather than in a pop-up, so a chat stays live beside them — you can ask Daimond what an app password is while the box asking for one is still on screen. Each row that can be acted on is a button that opens the thing it names:

  • An identity row — who you are on this device — with a settings cog beside it.
  • A Models row, which counts the providers you have connected and opens the Models form.
  • A Credits row, showing your balance and opening the Credits view.
  • A Tools · N of M row, showing how many tools are available and opening the Tools panel.
  • Workspace storage rows, showing how much space your files use, and — where the browser can open one — a real folder on your device.

The stage — centre

The stage is the middle zone. Its occupants take the stage on their own or two side by side, and never dock at the right. The point of two seats is that you never leave the conversation to do a thing: read the message, watch the page, read the document, with the daimon still beside it to be asked about it.

AI — the daimon

The conversation. This is the default occupant and it returns whenever the stage would otherwise be empty. Your messages, the answers, and any tool steps appear here.

Web

A web page, where a page can be shown or driven. Opened from the header or when Daimond follows a link.

Doc

A compiled document (such as a PDF), given room to read.

Message

A single mail message, opened from Email, read beside the chat.

Tools

What Daimond can do, and what the rest would cost. Opened from the Tools row.

Compose

Writing a mail message. Nothing here sends by itself — only the Send button puts mail on the wire, and only you can press it.

Each stage guest has a header with a close button. A closed panel does not vanish: its chip stays in the top bar, unfilled, and clicking the chip brings it back.

The dock — right

The dock holds the sources you pull from. Closing one leaves its chip in the top bar; clicking the chip docks it again. How the dock tiles, and therefore how many panels it will hold at once, is yours to set under Dock tiling in the appearance menu, described below.

Email

Your mailboxes and what is in them. Add a mailbox, sync, and open a message onto the stage.

Workspace

Your files, as an ordinary tree. The agent's file tools read and write here.

Agents

Live agent runs, as they happen, with a count of how many are working.

Spending

What you have spent, and on what. Covered in full on Spending.

The top bar

Across the top sit the brand mark, a meter, a row of panel chips, and a button at the far right that opens the appearance and layout menu.

The chips

Every panel has a chip, whether it is open or shut, and clicking one toggles that panel. An open panel's chip is filled in, so the row reads as a map of what is on screen. The chips are toggles rather than summons for a reason: if only the closed panels had one, the row would be longest when the app was emptiest, and every chip would shift sideways each time one was used. A chip that stays put is one you can learn the position of. (Agents is the exception to every: it keeps out of the way until the first agent runs.)

The chips are grouped by zone — rail, then stage, then dock — with a thin divider between the groups, running left to right in the same order the zones do on screen. So a chip sits on the side the panel it opens will appear on. Two marks are worth knowing:

  • A closed stage chip whose panel would displace the guest currently beside the chat is drawn with a dashed outline. The stage seats two, and the daimon keeps its seat, so opening a third guest means the other one leaves. The outline warns rather than refuses, because that is a real choice — and it is an outline rather than an added mark so that the chip keeps its width, and its neighbours do not shift.
  • A closed dock chip is greyed out when the dock is full. Its tooltip points at the fix: choose a larger tiling in the appearance menu, or close a dock panel.

Pinned panels, and the gallery

You decide which chips sit in the top bar. Any panel can be pinned or unpinned; the unpinned ones live behind a ⋯ chip at the end of the row, with a count of how many are back there. Clicking it opens the gallery: every panel there is, searchable by name, grouped by zone, each row carrying a pin control and saying whether that panel is open. Clicking a row opens or closes the panel as a chip would.

The row is allowed to be incomplete only because the gallery is not. Nothing is ranked by how often you use it, either: a row that reorders itself would destroy the muscle memory that fixed positions exist to build.

The palette

Ctrl K — Cmd K on a Mac — opens a search box that reaches any panel or any setting by typing. Arrow keys move through the results, Enter runs the highlighted one, and Esc closes it. It lists the panels, the three themes, the four text sizes and the dock tilings. It is never the fastest route to a panel you can see a chip for; it is the route that stays complete however many panels there are.

Appearance and layout

The button at the top right opens one menu holding the choices about how Daimond looks and sits, as opposed to what it does:

  • Theme — Light, Dark or Lollypop. This guide follows the app's theme when it is shown inside Daimond, reading Lollypop as a light background.
  • Text size — four steps, Small, Normal, Large and Larger. Only the type responds: the frame, the padding and the panel widths stay where they are, because this is a control for reading rather than a zoom. If you want the whole interface bigger, the browser's own zoom does that. The sample in the menu is set in the size being chosen, so the control shows the change rather than naming it.
  • Dock tiling — Auto, 1 column, 2×2, 2×3 or 3×2, each drawn as the grid it selects. The tiling also decides how many panels the dock can hold, which is its columns times its rows: four in one column, four at 2×2, six at either 2×3 or 3×2. Auto lets the window decide, taking a second column once there is genuinely room for one. Moving to a smaller grid closes whichever panels no longer fit, most recently opened first — they are closed rather than dropped quietly, so each goes back to being a chip you can see and click.

Keeping an arrangement with a Facet

When a Facet is selected, the appearance menu offers to keep the current arrangement of panels with it. Opening that Facet again puts the arrangement back, so returning to a piece of work does not mean reassembling by hand the panels it needs. It is saved only when you ask — never inferred, and never in the background — because a switch that silently closed panels would read as work being lost. The menu also lets you update a saved arrangement, or forget it.

On a narrow screen

Below the phone width the chips and the gallery give way to the phone shell, which answers the same question with less room: the chat is the floor, a panel rises over it as a sheet you can drag, and the rail becomes a drawer opened by the button at the top left. A row along the bottom — Chat, Email, Files, Agents — moves between them. Nothing is lost; it is the same panels, shown one at a time.
